Setting

"The Hungry Tide" is set in the Sundarbans, a vast and remote archipelago in the Bay of Bengal, spanning India and Bangladesh. The region is known for its dense mangrove forests, intricate network of tidal rivers, and rich biodiversity. The setting plays a crucial role in the narrative, influencing the lives of the characters and the unfolding of the plot.

Key Aspects

  1. Geography

    • Mangrove Forests: The Sundarbans is home to one of the largest mangrove forests in the world. These forests are crucial to the local ecosystem, providing habitat for diverse species and protecting the inland areas from storm surges.

    • Tidal Rivers: The region is characterized by a complex system of tidal rivers and estuaries. The tides significantly influence daily life, and navigating these waterways is a skill essential for survival.

    • Islands: The Sundarbans consists of numerous islands, many of which are constantly reshaped by the tides. Some islands are inhabited, while others are wild and dangerous.

  2. Flora and Fauna

    • Biodiversity: The Sundarbans is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, known for its rich biodiversity. It is home to the Bengal tiger, saltwater crocodiles, various species of birds, and the rare Irrawaddy dolphin.

    • Vegetation: The mangrove trees, with their complex root systems, dominate the landscape. Other notable vegetation includes Sundari trees (from which the Sundarbans gets its name) and Nypa palms.

  3. Climate and Natural Hazards

    • Cyclones and Storm Surges: The region is prone to severe cyclones and storm surges, which can cause devastating floods and loss of life. These natural disasters are a constant threat to the inhabitants.

    • Tides: The daily ebb and flow of the tides shape the geography and ecology of the Sundarbans. The tides can be both a boon and a bane for the local population, influencing fishing and agriculture.

  4. Human Inhabitants

    • Villages: Small, isolated villages are scattered across the inhabited islands. Life in these villages is challenging, with limited access to modern amenities.

    • Economy: The local economy is primarily based on fishing, honey collection, and limited agriculture. The people of the Sundarbans rely heavily on the natural resources provided by the mangrove forests and waterways.

    • Cultural Practices: The inhabitants have a deep cultural connection to their environment. They worship local deities like Bon Bibi, the guardian of the forest, and have unique traditions and practices shaped by their surroundings.
